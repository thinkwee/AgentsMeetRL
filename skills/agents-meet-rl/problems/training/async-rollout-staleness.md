# Problem: async rollout creates off-policy bias / staleness

## Symptoms

- KL stays low but importance ratios drift away from 1.0 over a few
  steps.
- `max_ratio` in PPO log spikes above 5–10.
- Eval gets worse even though training reward looks fine — symptom of
  off-policy bias misleading the optimizer.
- Throughput is high (good) but stability suffers (bad).

## Root causes

Async-rollout architectures (vLLM/SGLang separate from trainer) generate
tokens with a *snapshot* of the policy weights. By the time the trainer
uses these tokens, the weights have moved. The standard PPO importance
ratio `π_new / π_old` is large because `π_old` corresponds to a *stale*
policy, not the policy that actually generated the tokens.

If you store `old_log_probs` from the stale policy at rollout time, then
re-compute `log_probs` from the latest policy at training time, the ratio
captures *both* the off-policy gap and the actual update.

## Diagnosis

- Log `gen_step` (rollout generation step) and `train_step` per batch.
  Compute `train_step - gen_step`. Bound this. If it grows, staleness
  is creeping.
- `mean_ratio` and `max_ratio` per training batch. Mean should be ≈ 1;
  max should be < 5.
- Compare `old_log_probs` (stored at rollout) to `recompute_log_probs`
  (computed at train time using the same model that generated). They
  should agree within numerical noise on token-level. Disagreement =
  drift / wrong policy snapshot.

## Fixes

### Fix 1: bound the staleness gap

Cap how far rollout policy can fall behind training policy. Most async
implementations have a parameter for this.

> **AReaL** — github: `https://github.com/inclusionAI/AReaL`,
> paper: `https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.24298`, AntGroup/Tsinghua, date: 2025.5.
> Decoupled PPO with explicit staleness bound. Used by ASearcher.

> **prime-rl** — github: `https://github.com/PrimeIntellect-ai/prime-rl`,
> Prime Intellect, date: 2025.2. Multi-turn PPO/GRPO with async-rollout
> patterns.

> **veRL experimental fully_async_policy** — github:
> `https://github.com/volcengine/verl`, ByteDance Seed.
> Decouples actor rollout from training; configurable staleness bound.

### Fix 2: refresh `old_log_probs` from the same policy that generated

If your rollout was generated by policy snapshot at step S, store
`old_log_probs` computed by *that* snapshot, not by the current trainer
copy.

This is the canonical PPO setup but commonly broken in async pipelines.

### Fix 3: clip importance ratios more aggressively

Reduce PPO clip ε. DAPO uses asymmetric clipping for positive/negative
advantage:

> **DAPO** — paper: `https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.14476`,
> blog: `https://dapo-sia.github.io/`, ByteDance Seed, date: 2025.3.
> Reduces ratio blowup in long-sequence settings.

### Fix 4: TIS (Truncated Importance Sampling) or related

If the gap is small but persistent, TIS-style truncation keeps things
stable.

veRL exposes a `RolloutCorrectionConfig` for rollout correction
options including TIS-style truncation.

### Fix 5: use REINFORCE++ (no critic, less importance-ratio sensitivity)

REINFORCE++ doesn't use the same importance-ratio mechanism as PPO and
is less sensitive to staleness. Some teams use it specifically for
async settings.

> **OpenRLHF** — github: `https://github.com/OpenRLHF/OpenRLHF`,
> paper: `https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.11143`, date: 2024.5.
> Strong REINFORCE++ implementation; used by `openrlhf_async_pipline`
> (`https://github.com/yyht/openrlhf_async_pipline`) for async setups.

### Fix 6: synchronous mode for debugging

If staleness is suspected, switch to fully synchronous rollout for a
diagnostic run. If KL/ratio normalize, async was the culprit.

## When async is worth it

- Large model + large batch where rollout is the bottleneck (>50% of
  step time).
- Multi-environment + multi-turn where the env is slow.
- Long context where vLLM/SGLang dominates.

When sync is fine: short sequences, small batches, fast envs.
